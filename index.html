What this is

Diana's standards, without Diana in the room.

A lot of what makes this team good lives in Diana's head — how she reads a prospect, how she writes an update, when something needs to come to her directly. This system makes that portable. You don't have to guess what she'd do.

One starting point, every time

Every request — a new lead, a showing prep, a deal question — goes to the Orchestrator first. It reads the situation, figures out what's clear and what's missing, and hands you off to the right specialist automatically. You describe what happened; it figures out what to do with it.

Context travels with the work

When work moves from one specialist to the next, it carries a packet — everything established, everything still unknown, what needs to happen next. Nothing gets rebuilt from scratch. Nothing falls through the gap between steps.

Nothing reaches a client unreviewed

Every draft runs through Quality Review before it goes out. Diana's standards are written down in the system, so review isn't a vibe check — it's a real gate with four specific criteria your draft has to clear.

Your voice stays yours

Client messages go out under your name, so they have to sound like you. There's a voice profile for each agent. The communication specialist won't draft without one — that's what keeps your messages from sounding like they could have come from anyone.

The specialists

Know what to expect when you land there.

The Orchestrator routes you — you don't pick the specialist yourself. These are here so you understand what each one produces and what it needs from you, so nothing surprises you mid-session. Expand any card to see the details.

00

Where every session starts

Orchestrator

↓

What it does

Reads whatever you drop in, works out what's clear and what's missing, flags anything that looks risky, and routes you to the right specialist with a full context packet. If your request is ambiguous it asks one clarifying question before routing — not five, one.

What you give it

Whatever just landed in your lap. A message from a prospect. A deal update. A question. Describe it in plain terms — you don't need to format or structure it. That's the Orchestrator's job.

What comes out

A Handoff Packet — a structured summary of what's established, what's unknown, and what happens next. Copy it into the next specialist's session and continue from there.

01

Routed here when a new prospect comes in

Lead Qualifier

↓

What it does

Turns whatever you know about a new prospect into a structured lead profile. Six fields, each with a confidence level: budget, timeline, location, financing, whether they're selling something, and what's driving the search right now.

Why the confidence levels matter

There's a difference between what a prospect told you and what you inferred. The profile makes that explicit so you know what's confirmed and what still needs answering before you invest real time.

What comes out

A complete lead profile and a recommended next step — usually a first contact draft, which routes to the communication specialist.

02

Routed here for showing or listing prep

Property Research

↓

What it does

Builds a research brief for a specific property, neighborhood, or market question — comparable sales, pricing analysis, anything the client is likely to ask that you should know before you walk in.

What it won't do

Produce a generic market summary that could apply to any property. Every brief is specific to the conversation it's preparing you for. One sharp insight over five generic ones.

What comes out

A brief organized for a conversation, with a clear bottom line you can use in the room — not a spreadsheet to review before you go.

03

Routed here for any client-facing draft

Client Communication

↓

What it does

Drafts emails and messages in your voice — first contact, offer updates, inspection news, financing delays, anything that goes to a client. The output should sound like you wrote it on your best day, not like it came from a system.

The one hard requirement

It will not draft without your voice profile loaded. Set that up during onboarding and you won't run into it. Skip it and this specialist won't produce anything.

What comes out

A draft ready for Quality Review. It never routes a message directly to a client — review happens first, every time.

04

Routed here once a deal goes under contract

Transaction Coordinator

↓

What it does

Tracks every deadline, document, and open item from contract to close. Option period, inspection, financing contingency, appraisal, title, final walkthrough — each with a days-remaining count and a named owner.

How it handles risk

It surfaces problems before they become problems — not after a deadline passes. Anything that could blow the deal or needs Diana's judgment gets flagged explicitly for escalation.

Worth knowing

The checklist is built for Texas residential real estate. If a deal has different requirements, Diana will let you know and update the files.

05

Final check before anything reaches a client

Quality Review

↓

What it does

Holds every draft to four criteria before it goes out: Is it specific to this client and situation? Is hard information delivered directly, not softened? Is every sentence earning its place? Does it sound like the agent who signed it?

What to expect

It tells you what passes and what doesn't, and it says why. If something doesn't clear the bar it comes back with what needs adjusting — not a rubber stamp, a real review.

Where the criteria come from

Diana's Standard — a document in the system files that defines the quality bar for the whole team. If Diana's standards evolve, she updates that file and it flows through automatically.

Getting set up

Three things before your first session.

The setup is under an hour. Most of that is the voice profile — which is worth taking seriously, since it's what keeps your client messages sounding like you and not like the system.

1

Download the files

All the system files live in one place online. Download the whole folder and keep it somewhere you can find — you'll load files from it at the start of each session.

Use "Get the files" at the top of this page. On that page, click the green Code button → Download ZIP → unzip to your desktop. Keep everything together — the files reference each other.

2

Set up your voice profile

Open the voice/ folder. Copy the template file, rename it to your first name, and fill it out. Diana's completed profile is in the same folder — read it first, it makes the template obvious. Takes about 15 minutes.

Don't skip this. The communication specialist won't draft in your name without it, and that's usually the first thing people need.

3

Run your first real request

Open Claude. Load the five files listed below — these are your starting files for every Orchestrator session. Drop in something from your actual week. You'll understand the system faster by running a real request than by reading about it.

Your first session

What happens when you run a request.

The Orchestrator produces a Handoff Packet. You copy it into the next specialist's session. That specialist does its work and produces another packet. Follow the chain until the work is done. You don't need to keep anything in your head — the packet carries the context.

The basic pattern

Request → Orchestrator → Packet → Specialist → Packet → Next specialist → You review and act.

Why you copy the packet manually

You move the packet from session to session yourself — intentionally. It keeps the system inspectable. You can see exactly what carried forward and catch anything that looks off before the next specialist runs. No hidden state.

When Diana updates the system

If Diana changes how the system works — a new quality criterion, an updated checklist, a new voice profile for someone joining the team — she'll let everyone know directly. Download the latest files, replace your current ones, and your next session picks up the change. Nothing else to do.

↓ For Diana — setup & administration

Standing it up. Keeping it current.

Team members get everything they need above. This section covers initial setup, ongoing maintenance, and the things only you need to know.

Before the team uses it

The repo ships with your voice profile completed and placeholder agent names throughout the examples. Replace those with your team's actual names and make sure each agent's voice profile is filled out before anyone runs a live session.

Start with voice profiles. The communication specialist blocks without them — it's the first wall a new hire hits.

When your standards evolve

The quality bar lives in one file: DIANA_STANDARD.md. When something genuinely changes about how the team should operate, update it there. All six specialists reference it — you don't need to touch anything else.

Don't document the standard in more than one place. Two versions means no version.

Adding a new team member

Send them this page first. Then have them run one practice request through the Orchestrator using something real from their first week. Walk through the output together once. After that they're functional.

Do the voice profile together, or review their draft before they use it live. It's 15 minutes and it matters.

Escalation

Every specialist has explicit escalation triggers — situations where it flags work for your review rather than producing output alone. High stakes, unusual situations, borderline calls, clients where your relationship matters especially. Common thread: anything where a mistake is hard to recover from.

Each specialist's rules file lists its specific escalation triggers for that domain.

The transaction checklist

The checklist in 04_transaction_coordinator/rules.md is built for Texas residential. For deals with different requirements, review it before the team uses it on that deal type. The deadline tracking logic carries over — the milestones may not.

Worth a quick look before an unusual deal type goes through for the first time.
