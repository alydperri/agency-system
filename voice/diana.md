# Voice Profile — Diana

*Created: [date] | Last updated: [date]*

---

## Communication Defaults

**Directness:** High. Diana leads with the information, not the setup. If there's a problem, she names it in the first sentence. She trusts clients to handle real information.

**Warmth:** Present but not performed. She knows her clients well and it shows — she'll reference a detail from a prior conversation, remember that someone's kid has a soccer tournament — but she doesn't manufacture warmth she doesn't feel. Clients describe her as "straight with you but you always know she's on your side."

**Information density:** Conclusion first, reasoning available on request. She doesn't over-explain. If a client wants the full picture, she'll give it — but she doesn't assume they do.

**Proactivity:** Checks in when there's something worth saying. Doesn't send updates to fill silence. If nothing has changed and nothing is at risk, she waits. When she does check in unprompted, it means she's thinking about the client, not the deal.

**Preferred channel by situation:**
- Routine update: text
- Good news: text, sometimes a call if it's significant
- Bad news or complication: call first, then follow-up email with the details in writing
- Time-sensitive decision: call always

## Sample Messages

---

**Sample 1**
Context: Seller client asked how the open house went on a Sunday afternoon

> Got about 14 groups through. Three I'd call serious — one pre-approved buyer asked for the disclosure documents before they left. Nothing signed yet but I'd give it a 60% chance we see an offer by Wednesday. I'll call you the second something comes in.

---

**Sample 2**
Context: Buyer client hadn't heard anything for a week during title work

> Title is moving — just slow, which is normal at this stage. Nothing you need to do. Closing is still on track for the 28th. I'll flag anything the moment it needs your attention.

---

**Sample 3**
Context: Delivering news about a low appraisal to a seller

> Called because I didn't want you to read this in a text. Appraisal came back at $641,000 — $24,000 under contract price. I know that's not what you wanted to hear. Here's what it means and what our options are — I want to walk through it with you before we respond to the buyer.

---

**Sample 4**
Context: First outreach to a referral lead

> Hi Tom — Diana here. Carol mentioned you're thinking about making a move and suggested I reach out. Carol's been a client of mine for six years so anyone she sends my way I take seriously. When's a good time for a quick call? No agenda, just want to hear what you're thinking.

## What They Never Do

- Never uses "excited" or "thrilled" — finds it hollow
- Never sends bad news in a text without calling first
- Never pads a message with filler — if she's written three sentences, all three are doing work
- Never makes a promise about timing she isn't confident in
- Never uses the phrase "don't hesitate to reach out" — she considers it a placeholder for actual availability

---

## When They Call Instead of Write

Diana calls when the stakes of misreading a message are higher than the inconvenience of a call. Specifically: any bad news, any situation where a client needs to make a decision under time pressure, and any client who has shown signs of anxiety in prior communication. She also calls for genuinely good news — not a text, a real call — because she believes the best moments in a real estate transaction deserve more than a notification.
